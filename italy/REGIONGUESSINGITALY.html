Welcome to what is easily the most under-researched country in the Geoguessr community. Whether because of the large proportion of poor-quality coverage or lack of the more popular car and infrastructure metas, the Geoguessr community hasn’t paid much attention to this beautiful country, but below the surface is a nation rich in regional identity, floral diversity and natural majesty.

Welcome to Italy.

GENERAL

RAINFALL

One thing that’s very important to understand about Italy is the unintuitive way that rainfall is distributed on the peninsula. Calabria and Campania are surprisingly wet, which produces rounds that look weirdly northern, while the area in between in Southern Tuscany and Lazio is quite dry (especially around Grosseto).

TOPOGRAPHY

The Alps and Po Valley are already well-known, however it is worth learning Italian topography in more detail. Many unique topographical areas will be discussed in the landscape section, but notice how the highest mountains on the peninsula are in Northern Tuscany and in Abruzzo - these are the only ranges which have peaks above the treeline (outside of the Alps). It is also worth learning about the numerous large northwest-southeast oriented valleys in Tuscany and Umbria, as well as the numerous flat coastal plains in otherwise very hilly regions such as Sicily (south of Mount Etna next to Catania) and Sardinia (south around Cagliari).

GDP

Italy has a strong north-south divide with the north being much more prosperous than the south. It is worth bearing this in mind as well as more subtle differences as it impacts heavily on architecture and quality of infrastructure.

LANDSCAPE

DOLOMITES

The Alps can be hard to regionguess, however if you see particularly jagged peaks you can generally safely assume that you are in the eastern Italian Alps, specifically within this area:

(Note the ‘Dolomites’ is not the name of the whole range but the most famous and jagged part)

CENTRAL APPENNINI

The Appennini mountains is the mountain range that forms the spine of the whole Italian ‘boot’. In the exact centre of Italy you can find the highest mountains of this range, covered by numerous national parks from Monti Sibillini in the north to Maiella in the south. Treeless yet snowless mountaintops distinct from the Alps are unique to this area. Roads going through these treeless highlands are usually in the Gran Sasso area.

TUSCANY

The Tuscan landscape is very iconic, consisting of rolling hills with loads of cypress trees. You will also commonly find vineyards, stone pines, and sometimes sunflowers and olives here.

MARCHE

On the other side of the peninsula you’ll find a topography very similar to Tuscany’s. This area makes for tricky 50-50s, however they can usually be told apart by the lack of certain plants like cypress trees and stone pines which mainly grow on the western side of the peninsula.

EUGANEI HILLS

The Euganei Hills are a very good reference point within the flat north, marked clearly on Google Maps just southwest of Padua. The hills are very unique and conical, and can often be seen from quite a distance.

PALERMO

The Palermo area has quite dramatic topography in a relatively dry area. It is usually quite reliable to go Palermo if you suspect Sicily and see dramatic mountains, particularly around/from an urban area.

GARGANO

The Gargano - the only mountainous part of the east coast, is not an easy area to get, but good to be aware of. The only somewhat unique thing about it is the abundance of Aleppo pines on the lower slopes (Aleppo Pines are mostly limited to Apulia) and dense forests of lichen and vine covered trees in the highlands.

BASILICATA EXPOSED LIMESTONE

In much of Basilicata you can find these interesting exposed limestone formations which are highly distinct and localised. (This can be found in the hills of Emilia-Romagna too but in a far more green landscape).

DOLOMITI LUCANE

Named after the jagged peaks of the north mentioned earlier, the Dolomiti Lucane comprise very unique mountains and rock formations confined to a small area in the southern region of Basilicata. On Google Maps you want to hedge on the label ‘Parco Regionale Gallipoli Cognato… (full name: ’Parco Regionale Gallipoli Cognato, Piccole Dolomiti Lucane’) roughly in the center of the Basilicata region.

WESTERN SICILY

Towards the western tip of Sicily you will find a particularly bare landscape of gently rolling hills. Vineyards and wind turbines are more common here than in other parts of the island, and you will also find Baglios (large stone farm houses, often abandoned). At the southern end of this western tip (Marsala-Mazara area), the rolling hills transition to an extremely flat landscape where shrubs and olive plantations are more common.

TABLE MOUNTAINS

In Northwestern Sardinia you can find many flat-topped mountains with a slight ‘wild west’ feel.

MONTE ARCUENTU

Monte Arcuentu and its neighbouring jagged peaks create a highly recognisable Mordor-like ridge visible for some distance on the west coast of Sardinia.

AMALFI COAST

The Amalfi Coast is a famous and very unique stretch of coastline south of Naples on a peninsula that stretches west out from Salerno (the Sorrento peninsula). A south facing, extremely cliffy and photogenic coastline is guaranteed Amalfi.

DRY ALPS

The upper Adige Valley near the tripoint with Switzerland and Austria (as well as connected valleys) is the driest part of the Alps. Here you’ll find very short grass and few trees outside of the mountain slopes.

FLORENCE-PRATO-PISTOIA VALLEY

This valley is covered in industrial estates and even more uniquely, vast areas dedicated to growing/storing ornamental plants, something very distinct and unique to this area. Prato is the most Chinese city in Europe and many of the industrial estates around it have Chinese signage.

GUBBIO

The Gubbio plain in Umbria is one of numerous NW-SE-facing ‘sister’ valleys of the Florence one, and it’s particularly recognisable for the ‘bald’ mountain on the northeast side of it.

LITORAL ROMANO

The coastal plain south of Rome is very flat and surprisingly warm given how far north it is. A deadflat area with lots of palms, reeds, stone pines, eucalyptus rows and mountains northeast is a guaranteed Litoral Romano.

FOGGIA

Aside from the Northern Plain, the only other large flat agricultural area in the country is the area around Foggia. This shouldn’t be hard to distinguish as it is significantly drier, and contains far more fallow fields or olive plantations which are generally lacking in the north.

Around this flat agricultural area you will find very gentle but bare rolling hills, often with wind turbines.

AGRICULTURE

SUNFLOWERS

Sunflowers are an underrated way to regionguess Italy. They are generally found in a belt stretching from Tuscany to Marche, towards the top of the Italian peninsula, although they are a little more common to the eastern end of that belt. Sunflowers in a dead-flat area are usually in the valley around Pisa.

CORN

Corn is a less useful crop. It’s primarily only found in the Po valley which is generally free anyway. Just note that it’s more common in the northern half of the valley than the southern half.

ALMONDS

Almond trees are of some use, mostly they blend with areas of heavy olive production, however combinations of almond and olive plantations can be indicative of Northern Puglia rather than the peninsula. Almond trees look similar to olive trees but have more saturated leaves and bloom during the spring.

HAZELNUTS

Italy is the second biggest producer of hazelnuts after Turkey. These are largely grown in the central-west-coast regions of Lazio and Campania, but there is an area of particularly high concentration just southeast of Viterbo. The most distinct feature of hazelnut plantations is the multiple-stems emanating from one point.

ADIGE VALLEY

The Adige Valley is a unique area within the Alps, located within Trentino province. Here you’ll find huge amounts of fruit trees (mostly just apple trees but occasionally vineyards too) against an alpine backdrop, making this region very recognisable.

VINEYARDS

As the world’s largest wine producer, vineyards are found in almost every Italian region and may seem unhelpful for regionguessing, however some wine regions look rather unique, and vineyards in combination with other metas can be very helpful.

BAROLO

The Barolo wine region is extremely recognisable as virtually every inch of space is covered by neatly organised vineyards. Once you see it you’ll always be able to recognise it. If you find yourself in such a region I recommend hedging near the city of Alba as Barolo itself is a small village on the edge of this region.

VENETO-FRIULI

Vineyards in a dead-flat region resembling the Po Valley means you are most likely in Veneto/Friuli towards the eastern end of the great North Italian Plain.

VALPOLICELLA

North of Verano you can find the Valpolicella wine region (appears in a few municipality names too), which has these quite distinct terraced vineyards.

GREENHOUSES

SEA OF PLASTIC

Italy has a couple ‘seas of plastic’ (similar to the one in southern Spain). Most of these are along the southern coast of Sicily, roughly in these highlighted areas:

You can find another sea of plastic in the coastal plain south of Salerno. This one is easily distinguished from Sicily by the much greener landscape. You may also see mountains in the distance from certain angles.

The third large sea of plastic is on the southern end of the aforementioned Litoral Romano and the adjacent coastal valley around Fondi.

GLASS GREENHOUSES

Around Albenga (in Liguria) you will find a huge number of glass greenhouses that look very distinct from the plastic ones in other parts of Italy. You can also find these in smaller numbers in some of the valleys further west towards the French border.

POPLAR PLANTATIONS

Poplar plantations are a risky meta for the eastern ends of the Northern Plain, particularly Friuli, where they are particularly highly concentrated, although they are also common in Piedmont/Lombardy. They are very recognisable for their orderliness and thin trunks.

CHERRY TREES

Cherry trees in Italy are heavily concentrated around the town of Vignola near Modena. These are very recognisable for their concrete supports if not the droopy-leafed trees themselves.

CALABRIA OLIVES

While olive trees can be found in most of Southern and even Central Italy, in Calabria olive plantations look quite distinct, with these orderly hillside plantations that are often visible from a distance.

SHEEP

Sheep = Sardinia (if not then most likely Tuscany)

VEGETATION

BIRCH

Birch is a useful tree assuming you already know you are in Italy. It can be used to identify the Northwestern mountains of the country, which can be very hard to distinguish from other parts of the Alps. (technically it can be found in other parts of the Alps, but it will be far less common).

EUCALYPTUS

Eucalyptus is a very good clue for Sardinia, where it is far more common than anywhere else in Italy. There are however a couple of other hotspots on the Litoral Romano and around Caltanissetta, Sicily.

CORK OAK

Cork oak is another good indicator for Sardinia. The tree itself is recognisable when the cork (the bark of the tree) has been harvested, baring its distinctive red trunk.

PINUS NIGRA

The highlands of Calabria can be devastating bait rounds as they consist of forests made almost entirely of pine. Being able to distinguish these Pinus Nigra forests from Alpine or other mountainous woodlands of the peninsula will spare you great embarrassment, especially as Pinus Nigra isn’t really found in any other part of Italy.

PINUS PINASTER

Pinus Pinaster also has a very neat distribution in Italy, although it rarely forms full forests. Nonetheless, if you can identify them, you can safely assume you are in Liguria or northern Tuscany.

STONE PINES

Stone pines are a little elusive. Generally speaking they can be found in most parts of Italy but if you see large numbers of them, or roads lined with stone pines, you are most likely in Lazio, with stone pines increasing in density with proximity to Rome.

GIANT FENNEL

Giant fennel is a very recognisable plant that is generally found in the far south of the country (Sicily, Calabria and Apulia). Be careful as they can also be found slightly less commonly in Sardinia.

RIVIERA DELLE PALME

Palms are fairly common in many parts of Italy, but on the East coast they are essentially limited to a stretch of riviera in Marche between the cities of Cupra Maritima and San Benedetto del Tronto. Palms with sea to the east (or low hills to the west) should be here.

TREE POLLARDING

In Friuli and eastern Veneto, you will find many ‘pollarded’ trees lining the roads. This means trees that have been ornamentally trimmed (similar to ‘Colombian trees’).

MASTIC BUSHES

Masitc bushes typify much of Sardinian coastal areas. Note that mastic can be found all over Italy, but Sardinia is the only place it seems where they are often the dominant species. Fields of low, puffy, bright green bushes are generally a good meta for Sardinia. (in the flat south of Sardinia you can also find many mastic trees lining rural roads).

ARCHITECTURE

LIGURIA

Liguria has some of the most distinct architecture in Italy, consisting of larger, more elegant, more richly coloured houses, often with painted motifs. While not unique to Liguria, green shutters are almost ubiquitous in this region. The way these shutters open upwards (visible in most pics below) is particularly distinct for Liguria and Tuscany.

UMBRIA

Umbrian architecture predominantly uses these beige bricks that are fairly distinct. While not unique, seeing them in large numbers can be a good indicator of the region, although this style does also leak substantially into Marche.

BRICK

All-brick architecture is particularly prominent in Piedmont. The more buildings you see made of brick, and the darker and thinner the bricks, the more likely you are to be in northern Piedmont. In addition, the further west you go in the Padan plain, the older the buildings.

SLATE ROOFS

Slate roofs are a good way to distinguish the Northwestern Alps (not just Aosta) from Trentino-South Tyrol. Furthermore, different types of slate roofs can be used to regionguess more narrowly within the northwestern mountains.

In Aosta and valleys south of it, you’ll find roofs made of large, diamond-shaped slabs of slate.

West of Aosta all the way to the border between Lombardy and Trentino, you will find smaller, rectangular slate tiles.

In the Ossola Valley, you will find these very unique rocky-looking roofs with small, messy tiles.

Even messier example

PORTICOES

The city of Bologna is famous for its porticoes - covered walkways in place of pavements. These can also be found in most city centres in the area around Bologna, including in nearby cities like Modena and Ferrara, as well as smaller towns.

EASTERN HILL TOWNS

Older towns from southern Marche down to the inland parts of Campania commonly feature pavement consisting of small black tiles with white motifs as well as arched doorways supported by three blocks.

MARCHE-ABRUZZO

In these regions you will find thin buildings made of thin, often unevenly coloured beige bricks. The colour can look similar to Umbrian stone, but these are thin bricks rather than large blocks.

SARDINIAN MODERNIST

In many Sardinian coastal towns you can find this quite unique style of modernist architecture. Note also that these mini villas are very common here even without this style.

SARDINIAN STONE

In central/inland Sardinia on the other hand you will find this unique stone type on older buildings.

RAGUSA STONE WALLS

In Southern Sicily, you will find an abundance of bright stone walls similar to those in Apulia but in a much more hilly landscape.

ISOLA DI PONZA

The small island of Ponza south of the Litoral Romano has very unique architecture. Almost all the buildings are boxy with defined outlines along all the edges. Some buildings are also baby blue which is even more unique.

EASTERN MILE MARKERS

SR ROADS

Italy has three types of roads, SS (state roads), SR (region roads) and SP (province roads). For the longest time SR roads were unique to the Aosta Valley as it is the only region not divided into provinces. SR roads have since extended to other regions but there are some where they are more common than others and some where they are completely absent.

        

NO HUNTING SIGNS

‘Divieto di Caccia’ (Hunting Prohibited) signs have some regionguessing potential. Yellow signs are generally only found in Emilia-Romagna & Veneto (rarely Lombardy).

Red signs can be found in Lombardy, Liguria and Marche.

In Veneto, ‘No Hunting’ signs often carry the title ‘Art. 11’.

In Piedmont, signs often have a red line through them.

BUS COMPANIES

REGIONAL COMPANIES

Much of the Italian bus network is privatised and many of these companies operate on a regional/provincial level. The most useful ones are listed here:

  • AT (Autolinee Toscane) - Tuscany exclusive (the symbol is in the shape of Tuscany)

  • Tiemme - Southern Tuscany exclusive (Arezzo, Grosseto, Siena, Livorno)

  • Cotral (Compagnia Trasporti Lazio) - Lazio exclusive

  • Autoguidovie - mostly Lombardy exclusive

  • ARPA (Autolinee Regionali Pubbliche Abruzzesi) - Abruzzo exclusive

  • AST (Azienda Siciliana Trasporti) - Sicily exclusive

  • ARST (Azienda Regionale Sarda Trasporti) - Sardinia exclusive

  • SAF - Friuli exclusive but especially Udine province, part of F.V.G. S.p.A

PROVINCIAL COMPANIES

  • Granda Bus - Cuneo province exclusive (colours very distinct)

  • GTT (Gruppo Torinese Trasporti) - Torino province exclusive

  • ATV (Azienda Trasporti Verona) - Verona province exclusive

  • SVT (Società Vicentina Trasporti) - Vicenza province exclusive

  • APAM - Mantua exclusive

  • Agenzia Mobilità Reggio Emilia - Reggio Emilia exclusive (dandelion symbol)

  • S.A.I. Treviglio - mostly southern Bergamo province but also in Brescia and Milan provinces

  • RAMA Mobilità - Grosseto province exclusive

BIN COMPANIES

Bin collection is also regionally privatised and therefore useful if you can read the labels.

  • HERA - mostly eastern Emilia-Romagna and Friulia, with some spill in neighbouring regions, most useful company to learn

  • SEI Toscana - Unique to southern Tuscany, logo rarely visible but very recognisable thanks to the red hand

  • NET S.p.A. - Unique to Friuli-Venezia Giulia, recognisable circular text

  • Irpiniambiente - Unique to Avellino province, recognisable from afar as a line of small text with a circle in the middle.

  • ASM Terni - Unique to Terni unsurprisingly, very recognisable ‘roundabout’ pattern associated with the Cosp Tecno Service company

PROVINCES

Italian provinces are almost always named after the largest city within the province. They use two-letter abbreviations that can appear in several places:

  • Billboards
  • Trucks
  • Business signs

The full names of provinces can also be found on many signs, including Hunting signs (although rarely large enough to read) and occasionally road signs, and are also often found as parts of smaller town names (see below).

SARDINIA

Sardinia has its own language - Sardinian, although I’ve chosen to include it here rather than the Minority Languages section because while you will very rarely find bilingual signage, the language has made the standard place names in Sardinia incredibly recognisable.

Sardinian includes the following distinct features:

  • ‘tz’ and ‘x’
  • Much higher prevalence of doubled consonants, particularly ‘dd’
  • ‘u’ at the end of words (note that ‘ù’ can be found in word endings in other parts of Italy)
  • Place names ending in ‘s’ (not unusual for other min. languages but very rare in Italian)
  • ‘ei’ at the end of place names + other vowel combos that look out of place in Italian

Furthermore, in the north of Sardinia they actually speak a different language called Gallurese which is closer to Corsican. In far northern Sardinia you can therefore find place names starting with the articles ‘Lu’ or ‘Li’ (you can find ‘La’ as well which exists in the rest of Italy but not the rest of Sardinia).

In the far southwest corner of Sardinia the article ‘Is’ is incredibly common in place names and is unique to this area.

Also ‘Nuraghe’ are ancient tombs that cover the island and can often be found signposted.

LOCALITIES

In Italy, there are many different regional names for certain locality types. Finding these on road signs can often give you the region.

CONTRADA

Sometimes shortened to c/da. Contradas mostly replace ‘Località’s which are found in most remaining regions, so if you see Località you can rule out the regions highlighted in red. (Use this one cautiously as contradas can rarely be found outside of this region)

CASCINA

Sometimes shortened to ‘Casc.’ or ‘c/na’. Cascine are more common towards the western end of the Po Valley.

CASALI

Unique to Friuli.

CORTE

Not that common but indicates a pretty specific area south of Lake Garda.

CA’

Ca’ is common but less reliable. It’s very Venetian but has a wide eastern spread beyond that.

MINORITY LANGUAGES

Besides the already known French, German and Slovenian, there are many other regional languages worth bearing in mind. These are listed in order of usefulness.

FRIULIAN

Friulian is found on roughly 40% of signs in the Friuli region and is highly recognisable for a few key features: ‘cj’, ‘ç’, and circumflex and grave accents (e.g. ‘â’, ‘à’). ‘N’ at the end of words is also common, although this can also be found in Eastern Veneto (see ‘-on’ suffix above). On street signs you will see the word ‘vie’ instead of Italian ‘via’.

LADIN

Ladin is in the same Rhaeto-Romance subfamily as Friulian, but doesn’t really have any distinguishing features. The helpful thing about Ladin is that it is spoken in parts of Italy where German is also official, so the clue here is trilingual signage, which is unique to only a few valleys between South Tyrol, Trentino and the Belluno province of Veneto.

FRANCOPROVENÇAL

Francoprovençal is a large dialect area in France that extends into Italy, covering the Aosta Valley and the valleys immediately south of it (area marked ‘Piedmont dialects’ on the map below). Of course, in the Aosta Valley French is co-official and a helpful meta, but it’s in these valleys just to the south where you can find Francoprovençal signage, usually at town entries. There will always be ‘pais francoprovénsal’ written in the bottom right corner of these signs.

OCCITAN

Despite its wide spread, you hardly ever see signs in Occitan. They do exist however, so if you see bilingual signs in the Alps that aren’t French or German, scan for the ‘Pais Francoprovénsal’ logo and if you don’t see it, then go for a valley further south. (Occitan flags can be seen too)

ARBËRESHË (ALBANIAN)

In several municipalities in Northern Calabria you can find bilingual communities who speak Arbëreshë - a dialect of Albanian, as well as Albanian national symbols and the town name component ‘Albanese’. Albanian is mainly recognisable for the ‘ë’ vowel, as well as lots of ‘dh’, ‘q’, ‘j’ and ‘k’. (Note the Albanian municipalities in Molise and Sicily on the map below don’t seem to have bilingual signs).

MOLISE CROATIAN

There are three Croatian-speaking municipalities in Molise. The Croatian spoken here is a little different but retains the characteristic ‘š’, ‘č’ and ‘ć’ characters.

GRIKO

Griko is a dialect of Greek spoken in two separate locations in Apulia and the southern tip of Calabria. In each case it is only spoken in a handful of towns/villages.

RESIAN

Dialect of Slovenian found in one valley west of the border peak Kanin (mainly towns of Oseacco and Stolvizza). Lots of irregular features such as two dots on vowels (ë), letter ‘ś’ and streets names with hyphens.

OTHER

Cimbrian - a dialect of German spoken even further south mostly in individual villages in Veneto. Borderline impossible to use due to wide spread - just bear in mind that bilingual town entry signs with German-looking names but an absence of German bilingual signs otherwise suggests somewhere in mountainous Veneto

Mòcheno - a weird dialect of German spoken south of where you typically see bilingual signs. Sometimes appears on signs in a valley called the Bernstol. Mòcheno town names don’t appear on Google Maps but on signs they seem to mostly end in ‘Pèrg’.

Wals - dialect of German spoken in the canton of Valais, Switzerland but leaks across in a couple of valleys near the Piedmont-Aosta border

Algherese Catalan - literally only the old town centre of Alghero, Sardinia

Guardia Piemontese - one village in Calabria where they speak a dialect of Occitan

Celle di San Vito - one village in Apulia where they speak a dialect of Francoprovençal

Furthermore, most of the regions in Northern Italy have their own language (usually considered ‘dialects’ of Italian although this is not exactly accurate) which can rarely be found on street signs. Because they are rare, only narrow down the region, and lack standardised varieties with clear orthographic differences, these are useless, but if anyone is particularly interested in linguistic metas they could look into this further.

SURNAMES

In Italian towns it is common to find obituary boards where you’ll find many surnames - these have quite strong regionguessing potential, as surnames are quite regional within Italy.

  • Very generally, in Central Italian regions (Tuscany, Umbria, Lazio, Emilia-Romagna, Marche), surnames almost exclusively end in ‘i’, with surnames ending in ‘o’ being more common to the south and north

  • The surname Rossi is the most common in most northern regions, with the similar surname Russo being the most common in most southern regions. The border between ‘north’ and ‘south’ Italy is the border between Lazio/Molise and Campania/Apulia.

  • Greco is very common in the far southern regions of Italy where there has historically been a lot of Greek migration. The variant ‘Grieco’ is mostly found in Basilicata.

  • The prefix ‘De’ (meaning ‘of’ is mostly only found in southern surnames. ‘Di’ is the variant used in Sicily and Abruzzo (super common in Abruzzo), although it is far more common in the latter. You will also uniquely find ‘Da’/Dal’ in Veneto.

  • Sardinia has the least surname diversity in the country and its surnames are very unique. They display features typical of the Sardinian language as outlined in the Toponymy section. Other differences include the names being quite short and often ending in ‘a’, however because so few surnames represent such a big share of the population, here are some of the key names to memorise: Sanna, Pinna, Serra, Manca, Melis, Piras, Lai, Mura.

  • Surnames ending in a grave accent (à) are pretty much only found in Calabria.

  • The second most common surname in Sicily is Messina and it’s essentially unique to the island.

  • The surname ‘Esposito’ is the most common in Campania and particularly localised to the Naples area. The surname was historically given to abandoned children who were ‘exhibited’ for potential adopters in the city.

Here is a useful tool if you’re interested in exploring Italian surnames in more detail: https://www.italianames.com/regional-italian-surnames.php
